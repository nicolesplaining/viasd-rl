\begin{abstract}
We reimplement VIA-SD (arXiv:2606.12243) --- speculative decoding with a slim,
layer-skipped intermediate verifier $q'$ --- and replace its hand-tuned
$(\alpha_1,\alpha_2)$ confidence thresholds with a \textbf{learned per-token gating
policy}. We study three RL formulations (REINFORCE, GRPO, and a per-token-reward
variant) and their reward/credit-assignment design. On a GSM8K slice with a Qwen2.5
\mbox{0.5B$\to$14B} pair, the learned gate \textbf{dramatically outperforms the paper's fixed
thresholds} (accuracy $0.21$--$0.40\to0.88$--$0.91$) at fewer expensive verifier calls.
We also report honest negatives: plain lossless speculative decoding stays
Pareto-competitive at this gap, and the slim-verifier tier only pays off when the gate is learned.
\end{abstract}

\section{Background: VIA-SD and DIMR}
\textbf{Speculative decoding (SD).} A cheap \emph{drafter} $p$ autoregressively proposes a block of
$\gamma$ candidate tokens; the expensive \emph{target} verifier $q$ scores all $\gamma$ in a single
parallel forward and accepts the longest prefix consistent with its own distribution, recomputing
the first rejected token. This is \emph{lossless} (output equals sampling/greedy from $q$) and trades
extra compute for fewer \emph{sequential} large-model steps. Its single lever is the
\emph{acceptance rate}: each rejection truncates the block and forces a full-model step.

\textbf{VIA-SD's contribution.} The paper observes that many rejected tokens lie in a ``middle zone''
--- the drafter is close but not fully reliable --- and do not actually need the full model. It
inserts a \emph{slim intermediate verifier} $q'$ between $p$ and $q$, turning the binary
accept/reject into a three-way \emph{hierarchical} decision per token: \textbf{accept} $p$'s token
when $q'$ is highly confident; \textbf{regenerate} with $q'$ for medium-confidence (middle-zone)
tokens; \textbf{escalate} to the full $q$ only for low-confidence tokens. By resolving middle-zone
tokens at $q'$, it reduces how often the expensive $q$ is invoked relative to plain SD. The routing
is governed by two fixed confidence thresholds $(\alpha_1,\alpha_2)$ on $q'$'s confidence ratio
(reported best at $\alpha_1{=}0.5,\alpha_2{=}0.3$); the scheme is mildly \emph{lossy}, with a
tolerance $\alpha$ controlling how far accepted tokens may deviate. The paper motivates the
intermediate stage information-theoretically: in KL geometry an intermediate distribution can lower
the \emph{cumulative} divergence on the path $p\!\to\!q'\!\to\!q$ below the direct $p\!\to\!q$,
whereas total-variation (which exactly equals the lossless rejection rate) forbids this.

\textbf{DIMR (Dynamic Intra-Model Routing): how $q'$ is built.} Rather than a separate model, $q'$ is
a \emph{routed sub-model of $q$ itself}: a binary mask $z\in\{0,1\}^L$ selects which of $q$'s $L$
decoder layers to keep, $q'=\Pi_z(q)$, skipping the rest (each decoder block is residual, so dropping
it is an identity on the residual stream --- dimensionally safe). $q'$ \emph{shares} $q$'s embeddings
and output head, so it adds no parameters and only changes inference dynamics. DIMR is the
\emph{offline search} for the mask $z^*$: it minimizes an accumulated KL-style cost between $q$ and
$q'_z$ over a calibration window (the paper's Eq.~12), combining random search with Bayesian/greedy
refinement, keeping first/last layers and skipping $\sim$45\% of the middle. The cost is \emph{not}
raw $\mathrm{KL}(q\Vert q')$ but the Eq.~11 \emph{margin-violation} cost --- a two-term ReLU penalty
(acceptance-threshold $+$ residual-replacement, at $(\alpha,\beta)$) that is the quantity actually
governing the $q'\!\to\!q$ gate's rejection rate. The paper shows DIMR-selected masks beat random/
evenly-spaced ones; our faithful search confirms this ($\sim$50\% lower cost than evenly-spaced).

\section{Methodology (our extension)}
\textbf{Pipeline.} Drafter $p$ (0.5B) proposes a $\gamma{=}5$-token block; for each token a gate
chooses one of three actions: \emph{accept} $p$'s token (cheap), \emph{regenerate} with the
slim verifier $q'$ (a layer-skipped copy of the full verifier $q$=14B, medium), or
\emph{escalate} to $q$ (expensive). The block commits up to the first changed token, then redrafts.

\textbf{Learned gate.} The fixed $(\alpha_1,\alpha_2)$ thresholds are replaced by a tiny MLP over
\emph{$q$-free} features (drafter/$q'$ entropy, top-1 prob, confidence ratio $q'(v)/\max q'$,
$p$/$q'$ agreement, $\mathrm{KL}(p\Vert q')$, block position). Crucially, $q$ is \emph{never} a
policy input --- it is used only at training time to compute rewards/labels --- so the gate is
deployable without running the full model to decide.

\textbf{Slim verifier $q'$ / DIMR.} $q'$ skips $\sim$45\% of $q$'s decoder layers (residual blocks
$\Rightarrow$ identity, dimensionally safe). The skip mask is found by an offline DIMR search
minimizing the paper's Eq.~11 \emph{margin-violation} cost between $q$ and $q'$ (the two-term
ReLU acceptance/residual cost, not raw $\mathrm{KL}$); the searched mask is $\sim$50\% lower-cost
than an evenly-spaced one.

\textbf{Metrics.} \emph{q-calls/token} (full-verifier invocations per token; exact, hardware-independent),
GSM8K accuracy, and speedup. SD's win is latency, but measured wall-clock is dominated by a
$\sim$10\,ms eager per-call overhead floor; we report an \textbf{overhead-free bandwidth-model
speedup} (per-forward cost $=$ active-params$\times2$\,bytes$/$HBM-bw), cross-validated by a
measure-and-subtract estimate (they agree for single-tier methods).

\section{Reinforcement Learning for the Gating Policy}

\subsection{Problem framing}
Each drafted-and-gated token is a decision step: state $s_t$ = the $q$-free feature vector,
action $a_t\in\{\textsc{accept},\textsc{regen},\textsc{escalate}\}$, policy $\pi_\theta$ a 2-layer MLP.
Routing effects are largely \emph{local} (the action at $t$ sets token $t$ and perturbs nearby
context), which motivates a contextual-bandit view. All policies are \textbf{warm-started from an
imitation policy} (below); RL then refines on the policy's \emph{own} rollout distribution.

\subsection{Imitation warm-start and exposure bias}
An oracle that may consult $q$ labels the cost-minimal route reproducing greedy-$q$
(\textsc{accept} if $p$'s token $=\arg\max q$; \textsc{regen} if $\arg\max q'=\arg\max q$;
else \textsc{escalate}); the MLP is fit with class-weighted cross-entropy. Imitation alone
reaches only $\sim$0.33 GSM8K accuracy: trained on oracle-visited (near-greedy) states, it
over-accepts at its \emph{own} states where errors compound --- classic \textbf{exposure bias}.
RL's primary value here is fixing this by training on-policy, lifting accuracy to $0.88$+.

\subsection{Reward design}
The decoding goal is to reproduce the target model's output cheaply, giving two ingredients:
\emph{quality} and \emph{cost}.
\begin{itemize}\itemsep1pt
\item \textbf{Quality (dense proxy).} $\text{match}_t=\mathbf{1}[\,\text{emitted}_t=\arg\max q(\cdot\mid x_{<t})\,]$,
computed by running $q$ at every position \emph{at training time only} (not charged to cost).
\item \textbf{Cost.} Per-action latency in \emph{overhead-corrected} units:
$\text{cost}_t=(t_{p1}+t_{q'}/\gamma+\mathbf{1}[\textsc{escalate}]\,t_q)/t_{q1}$ --- accept $\approx0.1$,
escalate $\approx1.5$.
\item \textbf{Correctness (sparse, true objective).} $\text{correct}\in\{0,1\}$, GSM8K final-answer match.
\end{itemize}
We compare three reward forms:
\begin{align}
\text{REINFORCE/GRPO (trajectory):}\quad & R=\text{match\_rate}+r_c\,\text{correct}-\lambda\,\overline{\text{cost}} \\
\text{v2 (per-token):}\quad & r_t=\text{match}_t-\lambda\,\text{cost}_t+\tfrac{r_c\,\text{correct}}{T} \\
\text{correctness-focused GRPO:}\quad & R=\text{correct}-\lambda\,\overline{\text{cost}}
\end{align}
\textbf{The proxy gap.} \texttt{match} is a \emph{dense surrogate} for the \emph{sparse} true
objective (correctness). We measured the disconnect: a policy matching greedy-$q$ on 98.5\% of
tokens still scores only $\sim$0.72 GSM8K accuracy, because a few deviations per chain derail the
final answer. This motivates the correctness-focused reward (Eq.~3), which optimizes the true
objective directly; its ceiling is bounded by the $q$-free features (a confidently-\emph{wrong}
drafter token looks safe without $q$). A cost penalty is retained or it collapses to
``escalate everything'' $=$ greedy.

\subsection{Credit assignment: per-trajectory vs.\ per-token}
\textbf{Trajectory-level (REINFORCE, GRPO).} One scalar $R$ per rollout is applied to \emph{all}
$\sim$150 routing decisions: $\nabla_\theta J=\mathbb{E}[A\sum_t\nabla\log\pi(a_t\mid s_t)]$.
Crude --- a good and a bad decision in the same rollout receive identical credit --- and
high-variance.
\textbf{Per-token (v2).} Each decision is treated as a local bandit step with its \emph{own}
$r_t$ and advantage, exploiting the locality of routing. This is far lower-variance and more
precise; empirically it produces the smoothest training curves (Fig.~3) and the lowest cost/token.

\subsection{Advantage estimation: REINFORCE vs.\ GRPO}
\textbf{REINFORCE} uses $A=R-b$ with $b$ a \emph{global EMA} of reward across \emph{all} prompts.
This conflates per-\emph{problem} difficulty (easy prompts give high $R$ regardless of routing)
with policy quality, injecting variance.
\textbf{GRPO} samples a \emph{group} of $G$ rollouts of the \emph{same} prompt and sets
$A_i=(R_i-\mathrm{mean}_{\text{group}})/(\mathrm{std}_{\text{group}}+\epsilon)$, cancelling problem
difficulty with no value network; it adds a PPO-clipped surrogate (clip $0.2$, $K{=}3$ inner
epochs), a KL penalty to the frozen warm-start reference, and an entropy bonus. GRPO is the right
tool for the \emph{sparse correctness} reward (Eq.~3): the group baseline yields a gradient
precisely on the mixed-difficulty prompts where some of the $G$ rollouts succeed and others fail.
\textbf{v2} uses batch-normalized per-token advantages (contextual-bandit REINFORCE) with an
entropy bonus and gradient clipping.

\subsection{What we observed}
Fig.~3: \textbf{v2 per-token} is the lowest-variance and drives rejection lowest; \textbf{GRPO} is
noisiest (group-level binary-ish reward); \textbf{REINFORCE} is intermediate; all converge to
match$\approx$0.95. On the efficiency metric, GRPO pushed q-calls/token lowest among the
match-rewarded variants (0.162), while the v2 reward drove escalation to $0.12$ (fastest). Because
the match-based rewards are match-dominated, the resulting RL is close to \emph{on-policy imitation}
(it fixes exposure bias rather than discovering exotic routing); the correctness-focused GRPO
(Eq.~3) is the formulation that targets the true objective directly.

\section{Findings}
\begin{enumerate}\itemsep2pt
\item \textbf{Learned gate $\gg$ fixed thresholds} ($0.88$--$0.91$ vs.\ $0.21$--$0.40$, and $\le0.50$
fully tuned), at lower q-calls/token --- robust across masks and capacity gaps.
\item \textbf{RL fixes imitation's exposure bias} ($0.33\to0.88$+) by training on-policy.
\item \textbf{DIMR helps only the learned gate} (via\_rl $0.88\to0.91$); the fixed rule stays brittle.
\item \textbf{Credit assignment matters}: per-token (v2) is far lower-variance than trajectory-level
REINFORCE/GRPO and reaches the lowest escalation; GRPO's group baseline beats REINFORCE's global one.
\item \textbf{v2 unlocks VIA-SD $>$ plain-SD \emph{on speed}} ($3.53\times>3.30\times$, escalation below
break-even) but overshoots accuracy ($0.71$) at $\lambda{=}0.3$.
\item \textbf{Honest negatives}: plain lossless SD stays Pareto-competitive (the $q'$ tier overhead
doesn't pay off at this gap); a bigger \emph{verifier} (32B) hurts an accept-heavy policy (acc $0.33$)
--- a bigger \emph{drafter} is the indicated lever.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Caveats}
Accuracy at small $n$ ($24$--$50$) carries a $\pm0.06$--$0.10$ CI, so small accuracy gaps are within
noise (large ones are not). Speedups are an overhead-free cost \emph{model}, not a stopwatch on an
optimized engine (CUDA-graphs/static-cache were incompatible with the dynamic KV-cache loop). Single
task (GSM8K), single model family (Qwen2.5).
